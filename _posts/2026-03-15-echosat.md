<h2 data-nav="Recap">Recap and Motivation</h2>

This is the third and final part of the series on *Tree Canopy Height Estimation*. In [Part 1](/blog/2025/estimating-canopy-height-at-scale/), we introduced a deep learning framework for global-scale canopy height estimation, producing a 10m-resolution height map for 2020 <d-cite key="pauls2024estimating"></d-cite>. In [Part 2](/blog/2025/capturing-temporal-dynamics-in-tree-canopy-height/), we moved beyond static snapshots by processing monthly Sentinel-2 time series through a 3D U-Net, producing the first temporal canopy height map of Europe for 2019--2022 <d-cite key="pauls2025capturing"></d-cite>.

These maps, along with other existing global height products, provide static representations of forests at a specific point in time. While already a valuable resource, they cannot be used to estimate year-to-year carbon absorption. Forests absorb 3.5 Pg of carbon per year, almost half of anthropogenic fossil fuel emissions. As global carbon emissions continue to increase, precise monitoring of forest carbon *dynamics* has become essential for effective climate policy.

Some studies do provide temporal monitoring of forests, but they are often limited to detecting large-scale disturbances such as forest losses due to big fires. Small-scale height decreases from degradation, individual tree mortality, or forest thinning are significantly harder to detect. The few approaches that attempt year-to-year height tracking rely on single-year models independently applied to multiple years, combined with extensive post-processing to achieve temporal consistency. None of them is based on a model that inherently learns forest temporal dynamics, which leads to unrealistic fluctuations at the pixel level and poor temporal coherence.

ECHOSAT addresses this gap. We provide the first global tree height mapping approach at high resolution (10m) across multiple years (2018--2024). Our method combines a transformer-based temporal regression model with a self-supervised growth loss that enforces physically realistic growth patterns, while addressing the challenge of sparse and temporally irregular ground truth labels.


<h2 data-nav="Approach">Our Approach</h2>

### Multi-Sensor Satellite Data

We integrate multi-temporal satellite data spanning 2018--2024, combining six complementary sources into a unified input tensor:

- **Sentinel-2** (optical): 12 spectral bands at 10m resolution, captured as monthly composites.
- **Sentinel-1** (C-band SAR): Quarterly composites from ascending and descending passes. Radar signals penetrate clouds and provide year-round coverage, critical in tropical regions.
- **ALOS PALSAR-2** (L-band SAR): Yearly composites of HH/HV polarizations. L-band radar penetrates deeper into the canopy than C-band, making it sensitive to woody biomass and canopy structure.
- **TanDEM-X DEM**: A digital elevation model providing terrain information.
- **TanDEM-X Forest/Non-Forest mask**: A classification map to distinguish forested from non-forested areas.
- **GEDI LiDAR (L2A V2)**: Sparsely distributed height measurements from NASA's laser system on the ISS, serving as ground truth labels for 2019--2024.

All sources are harmonized to 10m resolution and combined into a single input tensor of shape 18 $\times$ 84 $\times$ 96 $\times$ 96 (channels $\times$ timesteps $\times$ height $\times$ width). The training dataset comprises approximately 3 million samples from roughly 13,000 Sentinel-2 tiles, totaling about 50 TB of input data.


### Temporal-Swin-Unet Architecture

<div class="figure-container">
    <img src="/assets/img/blog_img/echosat/architecture.png" alt="Temporal-Swin-Unet architecture diagram" style="max-width: 100%;" class="zoomable" data-zoomable>
    <div class="figure-caption">The Temporal-Swin-Unet architecture. An encoder-decoder structure with shifted window attention processes the full 84-month input stack. Temporal downsample (TD) layers in the encoder reduce the time dimension, while temporal skip connections (TSC) in the decoder use transformer-based attention to map encoder features to per-year outputs.</div>
</div>

To handle this spatio-temporal input, we design a new architecture called the **Temporal-Swin-Unet**. It combines the Swin Transformer (shifted window attention), the Video Swin Transformer (temporal processing), and the Swin-Unet (encoder-decoder for dense prediction) into a unified model for pixel-level temporal regression.

Key design choices:
- **No patching**: We use a patch size of 1$\times$1$\times$1, preserving each pixel at its original resolution. This is important because a single 10m pixel can already contain an entire tree.
- **Temporal downsampling**: The encoder progressively reduces the temporal dimension from 84 months to 7 time steps (one per year) through dedicated temporal downsample (TD) layers, while spatial dimensions are reduced as in a standard U-Net.
- **Temporal skip connections (TSC)**: In the decoder, transformer-based attention layers connect encoder features (84 time steps) with decoder features (7 years), ensuring each year's prediction is informed by all relevant monthly observations.
- **Two output heads**: A reference head (single convolution) is used during pretraining, while a prediction head (three convolution layers) is used during fine-tuning with the growth loss. The model outputs a tensor of shape 2 $\times$ 7 $\times$ 96 $\times$ 96, i.e., two height predictions per year per pixel.


<h2 data-nav="Growth Loss">Self-Supervised Growth Loss</h2>

A key challenge in temporal tree height estimation is that GEDI ground truth labels are spatially sparse (~25m footprints at ~60m spacing along the ISS track) and temporally irregular (no labels for 2018, and GEDI was inoperational from March 2023 to April 2024). Training a temporal model directly on these labels can produce predictions that oscillate unrealistically between years.

We address this with a **self-supervised growth loss** that enforces biologically plausible temporal dynamics. Training proceeds in two stages:

**Stage 1: Pretraining.** The model is trained with a standard Huber loss on all available GEDI measurements using the reference head. After pretraining, this head produces reasonable per-pixel height estimates for each year, but they may be temporally noisy.

**Stage 2: Fine-tuning with growth loss.** We freeze the pretrained backbone and reference head, then train only the prediction head using pseudo-labels derived from the reference head's outputs. These pseudo-labels are constructed as follows:

1. For each pixel's time series, we detect **disturbance events**, defined as a height decrease exceeding 50% AND more than 4m AND falling below 10m within a two-year window.
2. We fit a **constrained piecewise linear regression** to the time series, with slopes clamped to [0, 3] m/year.
3. If a disturbance is detected, separate regression lines are fit before and after the event.
4. The fitted values serve as pseudo-labels for the prediction head.

The result is a model that produces temporally smooth predictions where forests are stable, while preserving sharp drops where real disturbances occurred.

<h2 data-nav="Temporal">Tracking Forest Change Over Time</h2>

The main contribution of ECHOSAT is its ability to track forest dynamics over seven years. Due to the sparse temporal and spatial distribution of GEDI labels, a direct temporal validation with GEDI is not possible. Instead, we analyze whether the predictions exhibit realistic forest growth patterns.

The left part of the figure below shows predicted heights in 2018 vs. 2024, with disturbed pixels (height decrease > 5m) marked in red. The right part shows median height differences from 2018 to each subsequent year, binned by 1m height classes. The analysis reveals consistent growth across all height classes, with taller trees exhibiting slower growth rates, consistent with established forest ecology.

We show predictions and temporal changes for two contrasting regions: the Le Landes forest in France, which is well known for its intensive wood production and fast-growing tree species, and the Amazon rainforest. In Le Landes, the predictions reveal many disturbances, most likely caused by logging activities, and phases of regrowth. In the Amazon, the predictions remain largely stable, showing very little variation across the different years.

The self-supervised growth loss is critical for these temporal patterns. Without it, the model produces noisy predictions that oscillate between years, making it impossible to distinguish real changes from prediction noise. An interesting finding from our ablation is that the growth loss does not significantly change single-year accuracy (the pseudo-labels come from the pretrained backbone), but it dramatically improves temporal consistency.

<h2 data-nav="Conclusion">Conclusion</h2>

Looking back at the arc of this series: we started with a global static map <d-cite key="pauls2024estimating"></d-cite>, added temporal dynamics over Europe <d-cite key="pauls2025capturing"></d-cite>, and now combine both dimensions into a single, coherent product. ECHOSAT is the first global-scale tree height map that shows not only where forests are, but how they are changing at 10m resolution over seven years. We expect ECHOSAT to advance global efforts in carbon monitoring and disturbance assessment.

The maps can be accessed at [github.com/AI4Forest/ECHOSAT](https://github.com/AI4Forest/ECHOSAT).
